Conclusion and future work
In this work, we used the framework provided by FibLang to take a ï¬rst stab at describing
vocabulary acquisition mathematically. In particular, we deï¬ned the concept of explanation
to deï¬ne, in turn, vocabulary acquisition by paraphrasis. A clear direction of future work
is to use Deï¬nition 4.2 as the building block for a more general theory of communication:
intuitively, speakers communicate in a game-theoretic fashion, exchanging explanations and
using them to build meanings until they reach some kind of ï¬xed point, at which providing
further explanations does not result in building further meaning. This broader picture would
fully capture Wittgensteinâ€™s ideas regarding language games, and we consider it an ambitious
goal. With respect to this, a conjecture we are currently working on is to show how the
bidirectional, game-theoretic nature of interactions between two speakers naturally suggests
the use of self-dual categorical structures.
On a more practical standpoint, we would like to investigate a possible replacement
of the limit in Deï¬nition 4.2 and in Deï¬nition 5.6 with something more linguistically
sound: it is true that limits are universal constructions in category theory, and, as such, are
mathematically well-behaved. Yet, in strictly applied contexts it may be useful to experiment
with alternative deï¬nitions: for instance, considering a model of meaning where concepts
in the ï¬bres are images may be a sensible choice to experiment with machine-learning
algorithms that merge images together [13].
