The steelpan is a pitched percussion instrument that
takes the form of a concave bowl with several
localized dimpled regions of varying curvature.
Each of these localized zones, called notes, can
vibrate independently when struck, and produce a
sustained tone of a well-deï¬ned pitch. While the
association of the localized zones with individual
notes has long been known and exploited, the
relationship between the shell geometry and the
strength of the mode conï¬nement remains unclear.
Here, we explore the spectral properties of the
steelpan modelled as a vibrating elastic shell. To
characterize the resulting eigenvalue problem, we
generalize a recently developed theory of localization
landscapes for scalar elliptic operators to the vector-
valued case, and predict the location of conï¬ned
eigenmodes by solving a Poisson problem. A ï¬nite-
element discretization of the shell shows that the
localization strength is determined by the difference
in curvature between the note and the surrounding
bowl. In addition to providing an explanation for
how
a
steelpan
operates
as
a
two-dimensional
xylophone, our study provides a geometric principle
for designing localized modes in elastic shells.

While the role of localized modes in the acoustics of steelpans has long been known, the
mechanism underlying mode conï¬nement is still incomplete. Steelpan makers conventionally
outline each note with chisel marks and mode conï¬nement has sometimes been ascribed to this
grooving process [4,5]. This explanation was challenged by Maloney, Barlow & Woodhouse [7],
who proposed that mode conï¬nement is a consequence of the steelpan geometry. In a numerical
and experimental modal analysis, they found that a ï¬‚at circular note region on a hemispherical
pan supports localized modes, suggesting that conï¬nement is inï¬‚uenced by bowl curvature [7].
Later experimental studies using holographic interferometry reveal a number of normal modes
that are completely localized to a single note region [3]; the mode shapes are qualitatively similar
to those of a ï¬‚at plate with the same dimensions. In general, the modes are designated by a pair
of numbers (m, n), where m is the number of radial nodal lines and n is the number of nodal
lines perpendicular to the radial direction. The frequency of the fundamental, or (0, 0) mode
determines the pitch, and makers of the steelpan carefully control the geometry to achieve two or
three harmonic overtones. To visualize this, ï¬gure 1b shows experimental images of the (0, 0) and
(0, 1) modes of a C4 note, both displaying strong localization.
A similar mode conï¬nement effect is observed in a related instrument, the musical saw, which
consists of a strip of metal such as the blade of a regular handsaw. When bent into an â€˜S-shapeâ€™,
a number of localized vibrational modes emerge at the inï¬‚ection line, where the non-vanishing
principal curvature changes sign [9]. Recent work [10] quantiï¬ed how the localization strength
varies with the curvature gradient. Moreover, our analysis suggests that these classical conï¬ned
modes are topologically protected, analogously to boundary modes in quantum-mechanical
topological insulators.
Here, we characterize how localization in elastic shells arises from variations in the curvature
of doubly curved shells, and characterize the strength of mode localization in the steelpan as a
function of bowl and note curvatures. We model the steelpan as a thin linear elastic shell with an
inhomogeneous curvature, whose dynamics is governed by a set of coupled partial differential
equations. To understand the vibrational properties of the system, we solve an eigenvalue
problem for an idealized version of the pan geometry.
In addition, we introduce an alternative approach based on a generalization of the localization
landscape (LL) method of Filoche & Mayboroda [11]. Originally developed as a geometric
alternative to explain Anderson localization [12] in quantum mechanical systems, one computes
the landscape function associated with the system, whose peaks coincide with the possible
localization regions. In the scalar case, the landscape can be approximated by solving a single
Poisson-like problem for the same operator, a computation which is considerably less expensive
than solving the eigenvalue problem. The approach has been successfully used to analyse
localized modes in various systems described by scalar equations [13â€“16]. Our generalization
of the technique is applicable to elliptic systems of partial differential equations, which describe a
wide variety of physical phenomena, including shell structures. We demonstrate the method for
a steelpan-like shell with realistic note shapes.

3. The elastic shell model
To apply our generalization of the LL to the steelpan, we modelled it as an elastic shell, i.e. as
a three-dimensional structure which is thin in one direction in comparison with the other two.
As a result of the thinness, the three-dimensional equations of continuum mechanics can be
simpliï¬ed to the two-dimensional problem of ï¬nding the deformation of the shellâ€™s midsurface.
Shell theories can be seen as a generalization of the more familiar plate theories, which model ï¬‚at
bodies, to structures which may be curved in their rest state. Like plates, shells are much weaker to
bending (i.e. isometric deformations) than they are to stretching and shearing. For shells, however,
the membrane (stretching) strains are generally coupled to the bending and shearing strains due
to the curvature of the midsurface. This has a dramatic effect on the mechanics, allowing shells
to resist applied loads more effectively. The curvature of the midsurface also complicates the
analysis: in contrast to plates, the equations describing in-surface and transverse displacements
are coupled, and one must generally work in curvilinear coordinates. Apart from exceptional
cases, the equations of shell theory can only be solved numerically.
There are a number of different shell theories, each based on different physical assumptions;
see for example [22] for an overview. Here, we use a speciï¬c model known as the Naghdi shell
model [23], which allows for extensional and shear deformations of the midsurface, as well as
shear and bending deformations transverse to the midsurface. These kinematical assumptions
are the same as the well-known Reissnerâ€“Mindlin model for thin plates [22], to which the Naghdi
model reduces in the special case of a ï¬‚at midsurface. The choice of model is motivated by
ease of computation: shearable theories such as the Naghdi model require only H1-conforming
ï¬nite elements methods, which are relatively simple to implement and are available in many free
software packages. This should be contrasted with models that do not allow for transverse shear
which give rise to weak formulations with solutions in H2, and thus call for more complicated
H2-conforming or discontinuous Galerkin methods.
We provide a self-contained derivation of the Naghdi equations for completeness, following
[22], leading directly to the equations of motion in weak form, required to apply the ï¬nite-element
method. Since our goal is modal analysis of the steelpan, we limit the discussion to the linearized
theory and clamped boundary conditions. For simplicity, we consider only the case of an isotropic,
homogeneous material of constant thickness.

4. Steelpan model and numerical simulations
(a) Numerical methods
We discretize the eigenvalue problem, equation (3.17), in space using the ï¬nite element method.
As noted earlier in this section, the weak form of the equations admits solutions in the Sobolev
space H1. However, it is well known that standard H1-conforming ï¬nite-element methods suffer
from numerical locking when applied to shell models. This leads to overstiff behaviour unless an
exceedingly ï¬ne mesh is used. Several approaches to alleviate the problem have been proposed,
see [24,25] and references therein. While no method has been rigorously shown to be locking-free
for all situations, numerical tests suggest that many of the known methods can successfully treat
locking in the Naghdi equations. Following [25], we use a high-order partial selective reduced
integration (PSRI) method, a variation of the technique introduced by Arnold & Brezzi [24].
This method was shown in [24] to converge uniformly with respect to the shell thickness under
somewhat restrictive assumptions on the coefï¬cients in the Naghdi model. It has also been found
to perform well on standard benchmark problems.

rotations (Î¸i), and Lagrange ï¬nite elements of degree 2 augmented by cubic bubble functions

contributions, one of which is computed with a reduced integration. This way, one avoids the

5. Results
(a) Effect of geometry on mode localization
Figure 4a shows the IPR and LNR of the (0, 0) mode as a function of the bowl and note curvatures.
In the region Îºi > Îºo, both localization measures are small and approximately constant, indicating
that the mode is extended over most of the pan. Near the diagonal Îºi = Îºo, there is a sharp
transition from extended to localized modes which is reï¬‚ected in both the IPR and LNR. Three
representative mode shapes, corresponding to the indicated points iâ€“iii in parameter space, are
shown in ï¬gure 4b.
In the localized region, Îºi < Îºo, the two localization measures diverge. The L1-norm ratio seems
to increase monotonically with the difference Îºo âˆ’Îºi between the outer and inner curvatures. The
IPR, by contrast, is approximately independent of Îºi in this region, but increases with Îºo. This
difference can be seen by comparing modes ii and iii in ï¬gure 4b, which are both conï¬ned to
the note region with similar L1 norm ratios, but have substantially different values of the IPR.
Therefore, the IPR gives more ï¬ne-grained information about the shape of the localized mode in
the note region.
(b) Illustration of the vector landscape
The vector landscape and several lowest eigenmodes were computed for the two shell geometries
described in Â§4. The results for the single-note pan are shown in ï¬gure 5a. The norm âˆ¥Lâˆ¥of
the vector landscape is almost vanishing outside of the note region, where it has a sharp peak.
Based on this, we predict that the shell supports one or more localized modes in the note region,
and only high-frequency modes can have substantial amplitude in the outer bowl region. This
is conï¬rmed in the lower half of ï¬gure 5a, which depicts the two lowest-frequency modes of
the shell. The pan has several more localized modes which are not shown here. When a note of
the steelpan is struck, each of the localized modes is excited and contributes to the richness of the
sound. Steelpan makers strive to tune the notes so that the overtones are harmonically related
to (i.e. integer multiples of) the fundamental frequency. Intuitively, it seems that localization is
crucial to this process, since it allows the craftsman to tune each note individually, with only a
minor effect on the neighbouring notes.
Figure 5b shows the more interesting case of a steelpan with multiple notes of varying size
and shape. The vector landscape âˆ¥Lâˆ¥has several peaks, each located at a note region where the
curvature vanishes. The tallest peak coincides with the large, circular inner note, followed by the
peak at the rectangular outer note. As explained in Â§2, the landscape inequality (2.14) guarantees
that any low-lying modes must be conï¬ned to the union of the note regions. Motivated by the
stronger results which hold for the scalar LL theory, we ask whether each mode is localized to
a single peak of the landscape, and thus a single note. Indeed, this is exactly the behaviour that
we observe in this example. Parts iiiâ€“vi of ï¬gure 5b show, in order, eigenmodes 1, 2, 4 and 5 of
the shell. Each mode is strongly localized to one of the four notes of the pan, with an IPR of 99.7,
49.8, 95.7 and 131.4, respectively. We note that each mode shape is reï¬‚ected in the shape of the
corresponding peak of the landscape. Moreover, the relative heights of the peaks is an indicator
of the order in which the localized modes appear. For example, the lowest-frequency mode is
localized to the circular inner note, where the landscape has the tallest peak. The third mode (not
shown) is also located there.

by makers of steelpans since the early twentieth century, but a general quantitative theory for

problem for the steelpan, we ï¬rst generalized a recent geometric theory for Anderson localization
for linear scalar operators to a vector-valued version. We then used the resulting LL theory to

a Poisson-like problem rather than an eigenvalue problem. Our results show an interesting
connection between curvature and mode conï¬nement in doubly curved elastic shells: shells with

the strength of localization increases with curvature. It might be pertinent to note that our results
are size and material independent, as the geometric theory of elastic shells is only predicated on
having a large aspect ratio (R/h â‰«1). Thus they apply equally to micro- or nanoscale systems, and
are suggestive of applications to high-quality resonators [30]. More generally, our generalization
of the landscape theory to vector-valued systems opens up applications to a much larger class of
